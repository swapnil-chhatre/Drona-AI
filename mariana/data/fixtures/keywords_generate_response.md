This list of keywords and definitions is designed for Year 8 Mathematics students in NSW, focusing on the investigation and application of Pythagoras’ Theorem.

### **Pythagoras’ Theorem: Key Vocabulary**

| Term | Definition | Contextual Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 🔑 **Right-angled Triangle** | A triangle that has one interior angle measuring exactly 90 degrees. | 📖 "We used a builder's square to ensure the corner of the new deck was a **right-angled triangle**." |
| 🔑 **Hypotenuse** | The longest side of a right-angled triangle, located directly opposite the right angle. | 📖 "In the triangle, the **hypotenuse** is always the side that doesn't touch the right angle." |
| 🔑 **Pythagoras’ Theorem** | The mathematical relationship stating that in a right-angled triangle, the square of the hypotenuse ($c^2$) equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides ($a^2 + b^2$). | 📖 "We applied **Pythagoras’ Theorem** to calculate the length of the diagonal path across the park." |
| 🔑 **Pythagorean Triad** | A set of three positive integers $(a, b, c)$ that perfectly satisfy the equation $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$. | 📖 "The numbers 3, 4, and 5 form a **Pythagorean triad** often used by tradespeople to check for square corners." |
| 🔑 **Square Root** | A value that, when multiplied by itself, gives the original number; used to solve for side lengths. | 📖 "After calculating $c^2 = 25$, I found the **square root** to determine that the hypotenuse is 5 cm." |
| 🔑 **Terminating Decimal** | A decimal number that has a finite number of digits and does not go on forever. | 📖 "When calculating the diagonal of the square, the result was a **terminating decimal** of 1.4." |
| 🔑 **Recurring Decimal** | A decimal number where one or more digits repeat infinitely. | 📖 "The calculation resulted in a **recurring decimal**, so we rounded it to two decimal places for accuracy." |
| 🔑 **Irrational Number** | A number that cannot be written as a simple fraction; its decimal expansion never ends or repeats. | 📖 "The length of the hypotenuse was an **irrational number**, so we left it in surd form: $\sqrt{2}$." |
| 🔑 **Surd** | An expression involving a root (e.g., $\sqrt{2}$) that cannot be simplified into a rational number. | 📖 "Instead of using a decimal approximation, we kept the answer as a **surd** to remain perfectly accurate." |
| 🔑 **Algebraic Manipulation** | The process of rearranging an equation to solve for a specific unknown variable. | 📖 "Using **algebraic manipulation**, we rearranged $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ to solve for side $b$." |
| 🔑 **Substitution** | Replacing a variable in a formula with a known numerical value. | 📖 "By **substitution** of the side lengths 6 and 8 into the theorem, we found the hypotenuse." |
| 🔑 **Proof** | A logical argument that demonstrates the truth of a mathematical statement beyond doubt. | 📖 "Eddie Woo provided a visual **proof** to show why the area of the squares on the sides adds up to the area of the square on the hypotenuse." |

***

### **Teacher’s Note on Curriculum Alignment**
*   **[ACMNA184]**: The inclusion of **Terminating/Recurring Decimals** and **Irrational Numbers** is critical, as students will frequently encounter non-perfect squares when calculating side lengths (e.g., $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.414...$).
*   **[ACMSP284]**: When applying Pythagoras' Theorem to real-world data (e.g., measuring distances on a map of Australia), encourage students to collect their own measurements and discuss why their results might involve rounding or recurring decimals.
