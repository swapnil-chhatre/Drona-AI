This resource is designed for Year 8 Civics and Citizenship students in NSW, aligning with the Australian Curriculum (ACARA) outcomes for the study of law-making and legal systems.

### **Year 8 Civics and Citizenship: Key Terms**

| Term | Definition | Contextual Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 🔑 **Statutory Law** | Laws made by the federal or state parliaments, also known as 'Acts' or 'legislation'. | 🇦🇺 The *Marriage Act 1961* is an example of statutory law passed by the Australian Parliament. |
| 🔑 **Common Law** | Law developed by judges through decisions made in court cases, rather than by parliament. | 🇦🇺 Common law principles, such as the 'duty of care', are established through historical court rulings. |
| 🔑 **Criminal Law** | Laws that protect the community by defining certain actions as offences and setting punishments. | 🇦🇺 If a person commits theft, they are charged under criminal law for breaking the peace. |
| 🔑 **Civil Law** | Laws that deal with disputes between individuals or groups, usually involving compensation or rights. | 🇦🇺 A dispute between a landlord and a tenant over a bond payment is handled through civil law. |
| 🔑 **Customary Law** | The traditional laws and practices of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples, passed down through generations. | 🇦🇺 Customary law often focuses on restorative justice and maintaining harmony within the community. |
| 🔑 **Bill** | A proposal for a new law that is presented to parliament for debate and voting. | 🇦🇺 Before a bill becomes an Act, it must be passed by both the House of Representatives and the Senate. |
| 🔑 **Democracy** | A system of government where power is vested in the people, who exercise it through free and fair elections. | 🇦🇺 Australia’s democracy relies on the active participation of citizens to elect representatives. |
| 🔑 **Lobby Group** | An organised group of people who try to influence government policy or decision-making. | 🇦🇺 Environmental lobby groups often meet with politicians to advocate for stronger climate change laws. |
| 🔑 **Freedom of Assembly** | The right of citizens to gather together in public to express their views or protest. | 🇦🇺 Citizens exercise their freedom of assembly when they hold a peaceful protest in a city square. |
| 🔑 **Representative** | A person chosen by the people (through an election) to make decisions on their behalf in parliament. | 🇦🇺 Your local Member of Parliament (MP) acts as your representative in the House of Representatives. |
| 🔑 **The Constitution** | The set of rules by which Australia is governed, outlining the powers of the parliament and the courts. | 🇦🇺 Section 51 of the Constitution lists the specific powers granted to the federal government. |
| 🔑 **Direct Action** | Using public protests, strikes, or boycotts to achieve a political or social goal. | 🇦🇺 Students participating in a climate strike are using direct action to influence government policy. |

### **Teacher’s Note on First Nations Perspectives**
When teaching **[ACHCK064]**, it is essential to highlight that Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander customary law is not a singular, uniform system. It is diverse, deeply connected to Country, and based on oral traditions and kinship. 

*   **Key Concept:** Unlike the adversarial nature of the Australian court system (which focuses on winning/losing), customary law often prioritises **restorative justice**—focusing on healing relationships and restoring balance within the community.
*   **Resource Tip:** Use the *Australians Together* handout to facilitate a classroom discussion on how customary law and the Australian legal system can sometimes overlap or conflict, and why understanding both is vital for a complete view of Australian identity.
