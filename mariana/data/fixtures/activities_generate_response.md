This teaching plan is designed for **NSW Year 8 Science**, specifically addressing **ACSSU153**. These activities focus on the processes of the rock cycle, the timescales involved, and the classification of rocks.

---

# 🌏 Year 8 Science: The Rock Cycle Activities Plan

## 1. 🍫 The "Edible" Rock Cycle Simulation
*A hands-on, tactile activity to model the transformation of rocks through heat, pressure, and weathering.*

*   **⏱️ Duration:** 45–60 minutes
*   **👥 Grouping:** Pairs
*   **🛠️ Materials:** Starburst lollies (or similar), foil, heat lamp or warm water bath, heavy books (for pressure), plastic knives.
*   **📝 Instructions:**
    1.  **Sedimentary:** Students shave lollies into small pieces (weathering/erosion), layer them in a pile, and press down with a heavy book (compaction/cementation).
    2.  **Metamorphic:** Wrap the "sedimentary" block in foil and apply gentle heat and pressure (using hands or a warm surface) to simulate metamorphism. Observe the change in texture.
    3.  **Igneous:** Melt the lollies completely in a heat-safe container (magma) and allow them to cool at different rates (fast cooling = small crystals; slow cooling = large crystals).
*   **🇦🇺 Australian Context:** Relate this to the formation of the **Australian cratons**—the ancient, stable parts of the continental crust where these processes have occurred over billions of years.

## 2. 🧩 Rock Cycle "Choose Your Own Adventure"
*An inquiry-based research activity using digital tools to map the journey of a rock.*

*   **⏱️ Duration:** 60 minutes
*   **👥 Grouping:** Individual
*   **🛠️ Materials:** Laptops/Tablets, [Annenberg Learner Interactive](https://www.learner.org/series/interactive-rock-cycle/), [Professor Dave Explains Video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VByCLpj-I_s).
*   **📝 Instructions:**
    1.  Students use the Annenberg Interactive tool to trace the path of a rock particle (e.g., "Start as Magma").
    2.  They must document at least three "transformations" (e.g., Magma → Igneous → Sedimentary → Metamorphic).
    3.  For each step, students create a "Rock Passport" entry explaining the process (e.g., cooling, heat/pressure, weathering) and the estimated timescale involved.
*   **🇦🇺 Australian Context:** Use the [Australian Museum Rock Cycle Poster](https://media.australian.museum/media/dd/Uploads/Documents/4643/rock_cycle.0114556.pdf) to identify specific Australian rock examples for each stage (e.g., Uluru as a sedimentary formation).

---

## 3. 🔍 Virtual Fieldwork: The WASP Experience
*A collaborative research activity using digital fieldwork to identify rock types in their natural environment.*

*   **⏱️ Duration:** 60–90 minutes
*   **👥 Grouping:** Small Groups (3-4)
*   **🛠️ Materials:** [WASP Year 8 Rocks & Minerals Package](https://www.wasp.edu.au/mod/page/view.php?id=87), tablets/laptops.
*   **📝 Instructions:**
    1.  Groups access the WASP Field Experience App or the provided teacher resources.
    2.  Students "visit" a virtual geological site. They must identify three different rock samples found at the site.
    3.  Using the WASP classification guide, they must categorize their findings as Igneous, Sedimentary, or Metamorphic.
    4.  Groups present a "Field Report" explaining why their assigned rocks are classified that way based on their mineral composition and grain size.
*   **🇦🇺 Australian Context:** Incorporate the **Aboriginal Perspective** section from the WASP resource, discussing how First Nations peoples have interacted with and utilized these specific rock types for tools and ochre for thousands of years.

---

## 4. 🎨 Rock Cycle Creative Infographic
*A summative assessment task to synthesize learning about processes and timescales.*

*   **⏱️ Duration:** 60 minutes (or homework extension)
*   **👥 Grouping:** Individual
*   **🛠️ Materials:** Poster paper or digital design tools (Canva/PowerPoint), markers.
*   **📝 Instructions:**
    1.  Students design an infographic that illustrates the Rock Cycle.
    2.  **Requirements:**
        *   Must include the three rock types and the processes connecting them.
        *   Must explicitly label the "timescales" (e.g., volcanic eruption vs. slow tectonic subduction).
        *   Must include one "Real-world Australian Example" for each rock type (e.g., Granite for Igneous, Sandstone for Sedimentary, Gneiss for Metamorphic).
*   **🇦🇺 Australian Context:** Refer to the [Australian Curriculum Work Samples](https://v8.australiancurriculum.edu.au/resources/work-samples/samples/rock-formation-at/) to ensure the level of communication and scientific terminology meets the Year 8 standard.

### 💡 Teacher Tip for NSW Classrooms:
When teaching **ACSSU153**, emphasize that the rock cycle is not a perfect circle but a complex web. Use the **ASTA Connected Learning Experience (CLE)** framework to encourage students to ask "What if?" questions—e.g., "What if a sedimentary rock is never buried deep enough to become metamorphic?" This promotes the *Critical and Creative Thinking* general capability.
