This resource is designed for Year 8 Geography students in NSW, focusing on the consequences of urbanisation [ACHGK054]. These activities are scaffolded for an **advanced** level, requiring critical analysis, synthesis of complex data, and a deep engagement with First Nations perspectives on land management.

---

# 🌏 Unit: Consequences of Urbanisation
## Focus: Jakarta (Indonesia) & Local Country (Australia)

### 🏷️ Activity 1: The Megacity Paradox – Comparative Analysis
*   **⏱️ Duration:** 90 Minutes
*   **👥 Grouping:** Pairs
*   **🛠️ Materials:** *ABC Education: Falling under the megacity spell* video, ADB Research Article on Green Open Space, Venn Diagram template.
*   **📝 Instructions:**
    1.  **Watch & Analyse:** Students watch the *Falling under the megacity spell* video. They must identify three "push" factors (leaving rural areas) and three "pull" factors (moving to Jakarta).
    2.  **Synthesise:** Students read the ADB article on Green Open Space in Indonesia. They must create a "Consequence Matrix" identifying how the lack of green space affects: (a) Air quality, (b) Flood risk, and (c) Social well-being.
    3.  **Compare:** Using a Venn diagram, students compare the environmental consequences of Jakarta’s rapid urbanisation with the urbanisation of their own local NSW city/town.
    4.  **Advanced Extension:** Students must write a 300-word justification arguing whether "Green Open Space" is a luxury or a necessity in a developing megacity.
*   **🇦🇺 Australian Context:** Students reflect on how Australian urban planning (e.g., Sydney’s "Green Grid") attempts to mitigate the same environmental issues identified in Jakarta.

---

### 🏷️ Activity 2: Cities are Country – Mapping Urban Connection
*   **⏱️ Duration:** 120 Minutes (Research + Mapping)
*   **👥 Grouping:** Individual
*   **🛠️ Materials:** *Australians Together: Cities are Country* unit guide, local council maps, GIS software (Google Earth).
*   **📝 Instructions:**
    1.  **Conceptualise:** Students engage with the *Australians Together* concept that "Cities are Country." They must research the Traditional Custodians of the land where their school is located.
    2.  **Mapping:** Using Google Earth, students identify three "urbanised" sites in their local area (e.g., a shopping centre, a highway, a high-density apartment complex).
    3.  **Critical Reflection:** For each site, students must write a "Reconnection Proposal." How could this space be designed to better acknowledge or incorporate First Nations knowledge of the land (e.g., water management, native planting, storytelling signage)?
    4.  **Presentation:** Students present their findings as a "Urban Planning Audit" to the class, justifying their suggestions using the principles of sustainable land management.
*   **🇦🇺 Australian Context:** This activity challenges the colonial view of cities as "built environments" and encourages students to see them as living landscapes that still hold deep cultural significance for First Nations Peoples.

---

### 🏷️ Activity 3: The "Urban Future" Simulation
*   **⏱️ Duration:** 120 Minutes
*   **👥 Grouping:** Small Groups (4-5 students)
*   **🛠️ Materials:** Large butcher paper, markers, "Jakarta Data Pack" (provided by teacher).
*   **📝 Instructions:**
    1.  **The Scenario:** Groups act as urban planning consultants for a rapidly growing district in Jakarta. They are given a budget and a set of competing priorities: Housing, Transport, Green Space, and Industrial Development.
    2.  **The Conflict:** Students must map out their district. They must incorporate at least two "consequences" of urbanisation (e.g., pollution, overcrowding, or loss of biodiversity) and propose a mitigation strategy for each.
    3.  **The First Nations Lens:** Groups must integrate a "Cultural Heritage" zone into their plan. They must consult the *Australians Together* framework to explain why preserving connection to land is a vital component of urban health, even in a non-Australian context.
    4.  **Peer Review:** Groups "pitch" their city plan to the class. The class acts as a "City Council" and must critique the plans based on sustainability and social equity.

---

### 🏷️ Activity 4: Reflective Essay – The Cost of Growth
*   **⏱️ Duration:** 60 Minutes (In-class writing)
*   **👥 Grouping:** Individual
*   **🛠️ Materials:** All previous research notes.
*   **📝 Instructions:**
    1.  **Prompt:** "Urbanisation is often framed as a sign of progress, yet it frequently results in the degradation of the environment and the erasure of cultural connection to Country. Discuss this statement with reference to Jakarta and your local Australian context."
    2.  **Requirements:**
        *   Use evidence from the ADB article regarding environmental impacts.
        *   Use evidence from the *Australians Together* resource regarding the importance of Country.
        *   Evaluate the effectiveness of current urban planning strategies in both regions.
    3.  **Advanced Expectation:** Students must use geographical terminology (e.g., *urban sprawl, sustainable development, ecological footprint, Traditional Custodianship*) accurately throughout their response.

---

### 💡 Teacher Note: Assessment for Learning
*   **Formative Check:** During Activity 3, circulate and ask students to explain the *causal link* between a specific urban change (e.g., concrete surfaces) and its consequence (e.g., urban heat island effect).
*   **Differentiation:** For advanced students, encourage them to research the *Jakarta-to-Nusantara* capital city relocation project as a case study for "planned urbanisation" vs. "organic urbanisation."
